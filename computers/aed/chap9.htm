Christmas comes early...

    While the dust settled from the major changes within Atari with James Morgan's arrival, several other things occurred.   Atari's Consumer Electronics Division and Atari's Home Computer Division were merged together to become the Atari Products Group.    Software development still stayed mostly the same.     

    In early November 1983 Atari took notice of a small Silicon Valley start up, previously called Hi-Toro, now renamed Amiga.    Their new chip designs had been completed by some former Atari engineers who were now working there - Jay Miner and Joe Decuir.    These new chips were for  a new game system/computer capable system called "Lorraine" and Atari was curious as to the very close similarities of its design to previous work done by both engineers just a few years ago while working for Atari on its home computer chipset.

    On November 21, 1983 Atari and Amiga signed an agreement to discuss possible further business between the two companies and delivered to Atari specifications of its "Lorraine" system and its early business plan.

    Apparently the talks and exchange of information must have gone very well during the initial November meeting.    At Atari on December 20, 1983 during a high level budget meeting, in Steve Bristow's engineering log, he writes down the future product plan of projects for the Atari Home Computer Division, among them is the mention of a new system called the "Atari 1850XL: 68000 based clones of Amiga game player system."

    Meanwhile in December 1983 a group of engineers formerly with Coin-op had moved over to work in Atari's Home Computer Group in the Advanced Engineering Division.    Their first project:   "GAZA" --- 

From Bruce Merritt, part of the Gaza/Mickey Team :
"Gaza was a project that we had pitched to the Warner Communications people, 
and Manny Gerard (Warner Comm Board member) had seen the promise in our
ideas (he was probably the most human big wheel I had encountered in
that crowd).  Anyway, Gaza was a proposed hi-res color graphics
workstation based on the Motorola 68000; had multiple processors 
(3 as I recall, user, graphics, and I/O) and was intended to compete with the
Sun/Apollo  equipment that had been announced around then.  Our group
designed and prototyped a 2-CPU system with video board (the CPUs
were interconnected via an independent bus and had separate everything), 
got started on development of (believe it or not) our own OS but bought/ported
CPM-68K (also just released) to our hardware prototype in order to
develop/demo the graphics capabilities."
