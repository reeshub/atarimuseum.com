Back to Atari's "Grass" roots...

        Before we continue forward, lets take a step back to 1976...    Up in Northern California, at a design consulting firm founded by two former Ampex employee's - Larry Emmons and Steve Mayer, called Cyan Engineering.    Located in Grass Valley, CA. this would become Atari's "Think Tank" were many of Atari's most innovative technologies during its early years would be born.   Among these technologies would be a design for a multipurpose microprocessor based video game console codenamed "Stella".

        After the initial designs were completed, the project and some of its engineers (Steve Mayer and a new engineer named Joe Decuir)  would move down to Atari's Sunnyvale headquarters to finalize the design, have marketing create a plan and move it to a production phase.      At this time the project was put under one of Atari's new project leaders - a man by the name of Jay Miner.   As Steve Mayer recalls:

"Initially Jay didn't know or understand much about video processing

methods and technologies.   However, he was like a sponge and whatever we

had shown him he immediately picked up on, learned and things went

from there."

 

 

        With that the new "TIA" (Television Interface Adapter) chip was born and with it, the legendary Atari 2600 "VCS" (Video Computer System).     Not sitting on their laurels, the chip design team under Jay Miner's direction move forward to produce a follow up chipset for a next generation video game system with a chipset that would have applications also in within Atari's coin-operated Arcade division as well.      The design would be much too big to fit into a costly and expensive higher pin number chip, so instead it was broken down into several individual 40 pin packages.   These new chips would be:

 

POKEY (POtentiometer and KEYboard controller - also to handle sound)

ANTIC (Alpha Numeric Transactor IC - DMA control and some graphics control)

CTIA/GTIA - Colleen Television Interface Adapter/George's Television Adapter - Graphics IC)

 

        The team would consist of Jay Miner, Joe Decuir, Doug Neubauer, George McLeod and one other IC designer.       During the course of the chipset development, Atari took on a new strategy - to compete in the home computer field.    This new advanced system chipset would wind up being used specifically for Atari's new Home Computer line, and the POKEY audio chip would also find its way in many of Atari's coin-op Arcade machines over the following years.

 

        Trouble began to brew within Atari and within its IC Design leader shortly after this.      Through some fancy financial wrangling, Atari was able to make its new computer division look just barely profittable.    This meant that the big fat bonus' promised to its IC design team would not materialize.     To add further insult to injury, with the new computers rolling off of the assembly line, peripherals and software being developed and sold it was time to look forward...

 

        Jay Miner pitched his next idea to Atari management.    Now that they had established their new computer division and their current computers were selling well, it was time to begin work on the next computer design.     Jay wanted to look at a next generation system based around the work completed.   Taking the current chipset farther with larger memory, better graphics and resolution.   This would mean developing a 16bit computer system.     Management had just settled into this new venture and was content to see where, if any place, it might take Atari.    It would be too costly to now invest again in an entirely new computer design when they already had a design which seemed solid and useable.    The answer back to Jay Miner would be a resounding "No" and with that, it was time for Jay Miner to look elsewhere to make his vision a reality.
