Hi Ho, Hi Ho, its off to Hi-Toro we go...

 

 

        Jay Miner would part ways with Atari and in a kismet of irony, would join up with another disenchanted former Atari employee from Atari's consumer game programming group - Larry Kaplan...

        Before this would happen, Jay Miner was approached by a engineer who started a new chip design company called Xymos where he worked for a short while.  So with some stock investment in this new Silicon Valley startup Jay did some chip design.  In the first months of 1982 Jay received a call from Larry Kaplan of Activision.   Larry wasn't happy at Activision and threw the idea of he and Jay getting together to start up their own company on the table. 

        So with Jay's stock in Xymos and some finance from some East Coast investors they were off and running.    In an weird turn of events, Amiga would open a small office on Scott Boulevard in Santa Clara, CA.    It was Atari's original office of all places!

        So with a new office in place, they got a Texas millionaire to put up some money as he had a strong interest in being on the ground floor of a new video game company.   So, Larry would be in charge of producing video game software and Jay  had some idea's about designing a games console that could be upgraded to become a real computer.   The whole part about the game console being able to become a computer was keep quiet from investors.

        So Jay headed over Santa Clara from Xymos.   Hi Toro was chosen as the name for the new company.    However the investors did not like the name and eventually gave Larry and Jay the new name for their company -  "Amiga"      Reluctantly the name was accepted, and it turned out it was a good decision as the name was approachable and accepted by all who heard it.

        So now it was time to assembly the "A" Team....   Jay specifically wanted people who could see along side him his vision and to share his commitment and passion.    He didn't want people just looking for a paycheck.      Dave Morse, Amiga's new President would be one such person and the game console project reflected this - the project would be codenamed "Lorraine" after his wife.

        Jay Miner arranged an interesting working and compensation in the company:   "We worked out a deal whereby I got a salary and some stock and I also got to bring my dog Mitchy into work every day.  Dave did reserve the right to go back on that one if anyone else objected but Mitchy was very popular."    

        Mitchy was no stranger to the world of high-tech engineering...   He used to come to work at Atari when Jay was working there.  In fact Mitchy even had his own security badge as well.   The only other "non-human" to have a security badge at Atari was "Kermit" - a robot designed by the engineers at Atari's Grass Valley think-tank.

Jay Miner Commented about his time and work at Amiga:

        "Being able to take my dog to work set the tone for the whole atmosphere of the place.  It was more than just companionship with Mitchy - the fact that she was there meant that the other people wouldn't be too critical of some of those we hired, who were quite frankly weird.  There were guys coming to work in purple tights and pink bunny slippers.  Dale Luck looked like your average off-the-street homeless hippy with long hair and was pretty laid back.  In fact the whole group was pretty laid back.  I wasn't about to say anything - I knew talent when I saw it and even Parasseau who spread the word was a bit weird in a lot of ways.  The job gets done and that's all that matters.  I didn't care how solutions came about even if people were working at home.

"There were a lot of various arguments and the way most were sorted out was by hitting each other with the foam baseball bats.  The stung a bit if you got hit hard.  There was a conflict in the fundamental design philosophy with some like RJ Mical wanting the low cost video game (the investors side, you might say).  Others like Dale Luck and Carl Sassenrath wanted the best computer expansion capability for the future.  This battle of cost was never ending, being internal; among us as well as with the investors and Commodore.

"You go through stages in any large project like the Amiga of thinking "This looks great and it's going to sell really well", and then things go wrong and you just want to quit!"

The unique spirit at Amiga was such that people worked tirelessly on their various projects, remembering that the software was well on the way to completion before any silicon had been pounded into the graphics chips.  Carl Sassenrath was brought in to do the operating system and was asked at the interview "What would you like to design?".  He just replied that he wanted
to do a multi-tasking operating system, and thus was born the Exec which lies at the very heart of the Amiga."

Meanwhile.....

        In 1982 we would see the dawn of Hi-Toro/Amiga.    Meanwhile another startup company was already in the works as well.   This too would be filled with former Atari employee's.   In fact their new founder and President would be the former head of Atari's Home Computer Division - Roger Baderscher.   This other new computer company?   Mindset Computer Corporation.  They too would look to create a machine that would break the early computer graphics barriers and would create a semi-PC compatible using the Intel 80186 processor along with a set of new graphics chips of their own design.
