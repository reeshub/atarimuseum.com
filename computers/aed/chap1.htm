In the beginning...

   It had always been assumed that Atari was no longer making any efforts to vastly improve its home computer line of products.   Publicly, with the ill-fated release of the Atari 5200 in 1982 which was an over engineered version of the Atari 400.    It was designed to make it as incompatible as possible with the home computer software/hardware systems.     Soon to follow was the equally ill-fated Atari 1200XL home computer.   While it slimmed down and cost reduced the original Atari 800 configuration down to a single motherboard, it offered little in the way of major enhancements over the original 1979 Atari 800 design.

        In 1983, the public saw the release of two new computer systems - the Atari 600XL and 800XL home computers.     Essentially these were peer replacements to the Atari 400 and 800 computers and with their introduction came the discontinuation of the Atari 1200XL computer.    Again these new systems, while offering some minor improvements were still only incremental enhancements, not major leaps forward from the original 1979 designs.

        Two additional systems were announced which were the Atari 1400XL and the 1450XLD.     These were essentially versions of the Atari 800XL systems with built in features such as modems and voice synthesizers.   In the case of the XLD system, a high speed double sided/double density disk drive was built in.     These were stepping stone enhancements and yet again, nothing revolutionary in enhancing or upgrading the original Atari 800 design from 1979.

        Meanwhile, out of sight of CES displays, reporters and public view there was quite a bit going on within Atari's Advanced Engineering Division.     The 6502 Processor had been at the heart of nearly every Atari product since the Atari 2600 VCS and while it was a capable workhorse - it was showing its age, and its limitations.    It was time for Atari to make some changes...
