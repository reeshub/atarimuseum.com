Silver&Gold, Tong is On!

    New Years 1984 had arrived, and with it, a new year, new start and new projects.     Among them was a set of new chips laid out as part of project "Rainbow" - a set of new high-res graphics chips called "Silver and Gold."   Also an audio processor called "Amy."     Samples would be ready around March to begin integration into test systems.     This project was originally started under Atari's Corporate Research Group headed by Chris Jeffers and would eventually find its way into the hands of Advanced Engineering.

 

    Meanwhile, in the Home Computer group, a project rises back from out of the ashes... the Atari 1450XLD is brought back on deck and put back on as a product to complete and release in the summer of 1984 along with the Atari 1090 XL Expansion System.       The 1450XLD was redesigned from its original 1983 design.     The project was now called "TONG" and would incorporate several new gate chips for handling device selection within the system (Modem, Voice Synthesizer and Disk Drive(s)) and would potentially be able to handle more memory directly.     The new prototype motherboard design was huge by any standard of the day, close to the size of an IBM PC/AT motherboard in fact.

 

Elsewhere, another machine was getting a lot of attention.   Behind closed doors at the January 1984 CES a lot of whispering and rumors abound of an amazing new graphics computer by a company called Amiga Corp was being shown.   It was called "Lorraine" and was the talk of show.      Within those closed doors John Ferrand, Ken Nussbacher and other Atari management were meeting with Dave Morse, Jay Miner and other Amiga personnel to continue their discussions on Atari financing the development of this amazing system and to obtain usage of the system for possible use in both its video games division and its home computer division.
